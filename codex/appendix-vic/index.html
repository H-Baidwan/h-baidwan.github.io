Victorian English  ·  Elevated Vocabulary Reference

A non-exhaustive reference list of vocabulary consistent with the literary tradition of Victorian prose. Words outside this Appendix are not automatically disqualified. Each entry includes a clause example demonstrating the word within Victorian structure.

States of Mind

Reverie"she had slipped, without quite intending it, into a reverie from which the afternoon could not recall her"
Contemplation"it was in contemplation, not in action, that the weight of the matter made itself fully known"
Anguish"the anguish was not loud; it was the kind that occupies a room without announcing itself"
Disquiet"a disquiet had taken root in him, small at first, then occupying every quiet moment he permitted himself"
Perturbation"the perturbation of his composure, so long preserved, was visible only to those who had known him longest"
Serenity"a serenity had come over her that those who loved her found more troubling than grief"
Desolation"the desolation was not in the place but in the certainty that the place would remain when she did not"
Agitation"the agitation beneath his measured speech was perceptible only in the stillness of his hands"
Resignation"resignation, when it came, arrived not as defeat but as a long-postponed understanding"
Foreboding"a foreboding she could neither name nor dismiss had accompanied her since the letter arrived"
Torpor"the torpor of long grief had made even small decisions feel like expeditions into unknown country"
Exhilaration"the exhilaration was brief and therefore precious, as all things are that cannot be prolonged without diminishment"
Vexation"his vexation, contained with effort, expressed itself only in the precision of his subsequent silences"
Solicitude"her solicitude for his condition exceeded what the occasion seemed, to those outside it, to demand"
Trepidation"with a trepidation she had not anticipated, she turned toward what she had long claimed to desire"
Consternation"the consternation of the assembled company was expressed, as consternation often is, in complete silence"
Equanimity"she received the news with an equanimity that cost her, as equanimity always does, considerably more than it appeared"
Weariness"the weariness was not of the body but of the kind that accumulates in those who have hoped too precisely"
Rapture"the rapture lasted only as long as the ignorance that had produced it"
Stupor"a stupor had descended upon the household, thick as the grief that had preceded it"
Indignation"her indignation was righteous but impractical, as indignation tends to be when directed at the irreversible"
Compunction"compunction, arriving late as it invariably did, found him already committed to the course it counselled against"
Elation"the elation was disproportionate to its cause, which told him more about his prior state than about the present one"

The Physical World

Contrivance"every contrivance of the age conspires against the stillness the mind requires to know itself"
Edifice"the edifice, already old when his grandfather had known it, seemed to regard him with a settled indifference"
Precipice"he stood at a precipice not of stone but of consequence, and found it no less vertiginous for that"
Vale"the vale lay below them, indifferent to the season and to the small figures moving across it"
Firmament"she had looked, on such evenings, to the firmament for the reassurance that the earth withheld"
Threshold"at the threshold, he paused — not from hesitation but from the recognition that entry was irreversible"
Vestibule"the vestibule retained the cold of the morning long after the rest of the house had surrendered it"
Expanse"the expanse before them offered the particular loneliness of space without destination"
Canopy"the canopy of branches above them filtered the light into something older than the afternoon it came from"
Rampart"the rampart had been built to keep something out; it had succeeded in keeping everything in"
Aperture"through the aperture, a single column of light fell upon the dust of the unoccupied room"
Sepulchre"the house had become, by degrees and without declaration, a sepulchre for the life once lived within it"
Corridor"the corridor stretched before her with the particular hostility of spaces designed for passage, not habitation"
Parapet"he leaned upon the parapet and regarded the city below with the detachment of one who had ceased to belong to it"
Hollow"the hollow in the hillside had collected the morning's silence and held it past noon"
Abyss"the abyss was not beneath him but within him, and far less navigable for that"
Terrain"the terrain of his grief was unmarked; he moved through it without the comfort of having been there before"
Nave"the nave absorbed their voices and returned them as something closer to supplication than speech"
Hearth"the hearth had been cold for a season, and the room had taken on the character of what it lacked"
Enclosure"the enclosure offered safety of the kind that cannot be distinguished, from the inside, from captivity"

Human Conduct

Forbearance"the forbearance she had exercised for so long had been mistaken, by those who benefited from it, for approval"
Propriety"propriety demanded one thing; honesty demanded another; she chose, as she always had, the former"
Contrition"his contrition was visible and, for that reason, slightly suspect to those who knew him best"
Duplicity"the duplicity had been so long sustained that he had ceased to recognise it as such"
Rectitude"his rectitude, genuine enough in its origins, had hardened over years into something indistinguishable from severity"
Dissemblance"the dissemblance was accomplished with such ease that she began to doubt whether ease itself was not its own confession"
Reticence"his reticence on the matter was itself a form of testimony, and was understood as such"
Magnanimity"the magnanimity of his response cost him nothing, which perhaps explained why it was so readily extended"
Impertinence"the impertinence of the question was exceeded only by the accuracy of its aim"
Condescension"the condescension was so refined as to be almost indistinguishable from benevolence, which was precisely its intention"
Deference"the deference he showed was genuine, though it was directed at the position rather than the person who occupied it"
Candour"her candour, delivered without cruelty, was the more devastating for its absence of intent"
Insolence"the insolence was not in the words but in the composure with which they were delivered"
Circumspection"circumspection had served him well in every matter except the one in which it was most required"
Caprice"what appeared to be caprice was, on closer examination, a system too personal to be legible to others"
Obstinacy"the obstinacy he displayed was the obstinacy of the man who has been right too often to consider the alternative"
Intransigence"her intransigence on this point was the single quality that those who admired her could not admire"
Presumption"the presumption of the request was so remarkable that it silenced, for a moment, even its intended recipient"
Acquiescence"the acquiescence was swift and therefore alarming to those who had expected resistance"
Decorum"decorum was preserved throughout, which is to say that nothing of any consequence was permitted to be said"
Perfidy"the perfidy was not of the spectacular kind; it was the slow, domestic variety that leaves no single moment to point at"
Probity"his probity in financial matters was beyond question, which made the other matters all the more confounding"
Cupidity"the cupidity that drove him was dressed, as cupidity invariably is, in the language of necessity"
Dissipation"the dissipation had been gradual enough that no single evening could be identified as its beginning"
Indiscretion"the indiscretion was small but had the particular quality of small indiscretions, which is that they are remembered longest"

Time and Passage

Hitherto"what was hitherto unspoken had accumulated in the room until the air itself felt weighted with it"
Henceforth"henceforth, she resolved, the door of that particular consideration would remain closed to her"
Erstwhile"the erstwhile companion, now a stranger in all but name, passed without acknowledgement"
Interminable"the interminable afternoon had the quality of time that refuses to be consumed by the activities arranged to consume it"
Transient"the happiness was transient in the manner of all happinesses that are recognised as such while they persist"
Perpetual"the perpetual noise of the age had made silence not merely rare but, to certain constitutions, unbearable"
Antecedent"the antecedent to the present misery was not a single event but a sequence so gradual as to be almost invisible"
Contemporaneous"the two events, though contemporaneous, were connected by something more deliberate than coincidence"
Intervening"the intervening years had softened the memory without altering its essential character"
Posterity"he wrote with an awareness of posterity that those who read him in his lifetime found faintly presumptuous"
Epoch"the epoch demanded a kind of certainty that the most honest among them found themselves unable to supply"
Vestige"the vestige of her former confidence remained in her posture long after it had abandoned her expression"
Remnant"the remnant of his dignity, small but intact, was the only thing he left the room with"
Precipitate"the precipitate departure had left the household in a confusion it took several days to resolve"
Immemorial"the custom was of immemorial origin, which is to say that no one living could recall either its beginning or its purpose"
Evanescent"the evanescent quality of the light at that hour made the garden appear to belong to a different season"
Inexorable"the inexorable passage of the matter toward its conclusion could be observed but not interrupted"
Protracted"the protracted nature of the farewell exceeded the emotional resources of those required to sustain it"
Lingering"a lingering sense of what had been said, or left unsaid, accompanied her through the remainder of the evening"
Imminent"the imminent conclusion of the affair cast a peculiar light over everything that preceded it"

Judgment and Reason

Discernment"the discernment she brought to matters of character was not always extended to matters of her own"
Conjecture"the conjecture, offered with the confidence of established fact, was received as such by those predisposed to believe it"
Fallacy"the fallacy at the centre of the argument had been visible from the outset to all who had no investment in its conclusion"
Prudence"prudence counselled silence; something older and less advisable counselled otherwise"
Veracity"the veracity of the account was not in question; it was its completeness that gave cause for reservation"
Perspicacity"the perspicacity she brought to others' difficulties abandoned her entirely when applied to her own"
Acuity"the acuity of his observation was in inverse proportion to his willingness to act upon what it revealed"
Sophistry"the sophistry was elegant and, to those who found elegant reasoning more persuasive than sound reasoning, entirely convincing"
Inference"the inference was reasonable but had the misfortune of being incorrect, which no degree of reasonableness can entirely excuse"
Deliberation"the deliberation was lengthy, which those who awaited its conclusion took as a sign of care rather than uncertainty"
Ratiocination"the ratiocination that had served him in every lesser matter failed him now at the point of greatest consequence"
Apprehension"his apprehension of the difficulty was complete; his willingness to address it was another matter entirely"
Postulation"the postulation was offered tentatively, in the manner of those who have already decided but wish to appear undecided"
Sagacity"the sagacity attributed to him was largely the product of a reputation formed before the evidence was available"
Lucidity"the lucidity of her argument was undeniable and therefore, to those who opposed it, deeply inconvenient"
Equivocation"the equivocation was so accomplished that it satisfied, temporarily, both sides of the question it declined to answer"
Cogitation"the cogitation lasted through the night and produced, by morning, a conclusion he had held since the previous afternoon"
Abstraction"the abstraction into which he retreated during moments of difficulty had become, over time, his primary residence"
Precept"the precept had been followed so long it had acquired the authority of instinct rather than instruction"
Erudition"his erudition was genuine and extensive and served, on certain occasions, as a substitute for understanding"

Loss and Absence

Bereft"she was bereft not of him but of the version of herself that had existed in relation to him"
Languish"the correspondence had been allowed to languish, and with it the understanding that had depended upon it"
Privation"the privation was material but its effects were felt most acutely in the curtailment of what had previously been taken for granted"
Lament"the lament, private and sustained, had been conducted without witnesses, which made it no less thorough"
Deprivation"the deprivation of company had sharpened her, in some respects, and diminished her in others"
Bereavement"the bereavement had altered his relationship to time in ways he could not have predicted and could not reverse"
Destitution"the destitution was of the spirit rather than the purse, which made it both harder to name and harder to remedy"
Forfeit"what had been forfeited in the transaction was not immediately apparent, and was not apparent at all until it was too late"
Void"the void left by the departure was disproportionate to what, examined plainly, the presence had actually provided"
Severance"the severance was clean, which is to say it was performed with precision and felt like violence"
Diminishment"the diminishment of his expectations had been so gradual that he had not noticed it until the expectations were gone entirely"
Abandonment"the abandonment was not dramatic; it was the slow withdrawal of attention, which is the more thorough kind"
Estrangement"the estrangement had occurred without a declared occasion, which made it impossible to appeal"
Dissolution"the dissolution of the arrangement had been so long anticipated that its arrival produced, in most, only relief"
Impoverishment"the impoverishment of the imagination was the consequence, she believed, of having been too long comfortable"

Power and Authority

Dominion"the dominion he exercised over the household was absolute and, for that reason, rarely required to be exercised at all"
Jurisdiction"the courtroom, filled of paperwork from centuries ago, a verdict on its run, delivered by none other than the Jurisdiction"
Sovereignty"the sovereignty of her own judgment was the only sovereignty she had never been persuaded to surrender"
Incumbency"the incumbency of the position carried obligations that its previous occupant had found convenient to overlook"
Prerogative"she exercised the prerogative of silence with the same authority others brought to speech"
Subjugation"the subjugation was so long established that the subjugated had ceased to experience it as such"
Ascendancy"the ascendancy of one faction over another had been so gradual that no single moment of defeat could be identified"
Edict"the edict was delivered without ceremony, which communicated, more effectively than ceremony could have, its finality"
Imperious"the imperious manner in which the instruction was delivered ensured that its content was almost entirely overlooked"
Hegemony"the hegemony had been maintained not by force but by the more durable instrument of assumed consent"
Mandate"the mandate was broad enough to accommodate whatever he had already decided to do within it"
Ordinance"the ordinance had been observed for so long that its origin had passed beyond the reach of institutional memory"
Suzerainty"the suzerainty exercised over the province was nominal in law and absolute in practice"
Usurpation"the usurpation was accomplished so quietly that those displaced were uncertain, for some time, whether it had occurred"
Tyranny"the tyranny was of the benevolent variety, which is the most complete because it permits no legitimate grievance"
Capitulation"the capitulation was dressed as compromise, which satisfied the dignity of all parties without altering the outcome"
Censure"the censure was public and therefore served a purpose beyond the correction of the censured"
Dispensation"the dispensation was granted with the reluctance of those who grant it only when refusal has become impossible"

Nature and Elements

Tempest"the tempest had passed by morning, leaving a stillness that felt less like calm than like held breath"
Vapour"the vapour rising from the valley obscured the lower reaches of the hill and gave the upper portions the appearance of suspension"
Verdure"the verdure of the hillside in that season was a reproach to the interior life she had been conducting"
Obscurity"the obscurity into which the figure retreated was not the obscurity of darkness but of distance"
Luminescence"the luminescence of the water at that hour suggested an internal source rather than a borrowed one"
Canticle"the canticle of the wind through the upper branches was the only sound the forest consented to produce"
Torrent"the torrent carried with it everything that had been too loosely held to the bank"
Penumbra"she stood at the penumbra of the lamplight, neither fully illuminated nor fully absent from the scene"
Canopy"the canopy of cloud had settled so low that the hills appeared to have grown since morning"
Sublime"the sublime indifference of the landscape to the small event occurring within it was, in its way, a comfort"
Ethereal"the ethereal quality of the light in that hour made the familiar landscape appear to belong to an account of itself"
Resplendent"the resplendent indifference of the morning to the night that had preceded it struck her as a form of cruelty"
Crepuscular"the crepuscular hour had always suited him, being neither the demand of day nor the abandonment of night"
Elemental"the grief had become elemental in the way that long griefs do — part of the weather of the person"
Meridian"at the meridian of the day, when shadow and light reached their most precise agreement, she made her decision"

Speech and Expression

Utterance"the utterance, once made, could not be recalled; it remained in the room long after the conversation had ended"
Proclamation"the proclamation was made with the conviction of one who had been rehearsing it privately for some considerable time"
Invective"the invective was delivered calmly, which made it considerably more effective than passion would have allowed"
Remonstrance"the remonstrance was heard, acknowledged with apparent attention, and entirely disregarded"
Imprecation"the imprecation was whispered rather than spoken, as though volume might make it more difficult to retract"
Soliloquy"the soliloquy, nominally addressed to the company, was in truth addressed to a single pair of ears within it"
Elegy"what had begun as a letter of explanation had become, by its third page, an elegy"
Exposition"the exposition was thorough and, in its thoroughness, revealed more than the speaker had intended"
Effusion"the effusion of feeling, genuine as it was, made those present uncertain how to arrange their own expressions"
Cadence"the cadence of her speech had the quality of thought arriving in real time rather than being delivered from preparation"
Discourse"the discourse had been elevated in its subject and disappointing in its conclusions, as elevated discourse often is"
Pronouncement"the pronouncement was final in its form and provisional, as all pronouncements are, in its effect"
Admonition"the admonition was delivered with sufficient gentleness to be ignored and sufficient precision to be remembered"
Supplication"the supplication was framed as a request but possessed the gravity of something closer to necessity"
Dissertation"what had been requested as an opinion arrived as a dissertation, which was not entirely unexpected"

Obligation and Duty

Incumbency"the incumbency of care fell to her by default, as it tends to fall to those who do not decline it quickly enough"
Compulsion"the compulsion he felt was not legal but moral, which made it, in certain respects, harder to resist"
Exigency"the exigency of the situation demanded action of the kind he had always preferred to defer"
Imperative"the imperative was clear; the capacity to meet it was the matter in question"
Onus"the onus had been transferred with such dexterity that the recipient had not noticed its arrival"
Fealty"the fealty he owed was to the principle rather than the person, a distinction that had cost him the person"
Injunction"the injunction was observed in its letter and systematically violated in its spirit"
Remit"the remit of her concern extended further than its authority, which is the common condition of those who care deeply"
Obligation"the obligation was of the kind that cannot be discharged but only carried, with varying degrees of grace"
Conscription"the conscription of her sympathy had been accomplished so gradually that she had not noticed it until it was complete"
Stipulation"the stipulation was attached to the offer with such apparent casualness that its significance was initially overlooked"
Proscription"the proscription covered precisely those activities that had made the arrangement worth entering into"

Desire and Longing

Yearning"the yearning was not for the thing itself but for the version of herself that had existed when the thing was possible"
Ardour"the ardour of his early intention had cooled into something more durable but considerably less useful"
Coveting"what had begun as admiration had, without her noticing the transition, become coveting"
Longing"the longing was retrospective, which made it both more precise and entirely without remedy"
Aspiration"the aspiration had been noble in its origin and had become, through the passage of time, merely habitual"
Appetite"the appetite for certainty, unmet for so long, had begun to accept probability as a reasonable substitute"
Predilection"her predilection for the melancholy in music was not, as some supposed, a pose; it was a preference formed before she had language for it"
Inclination"the inclination toward solitude had been present in him since childhood and had only strengthened with the provision of its alternatives"
Pining"the pining was conducted without exhibition, which is the only variety that lasts"
Craving"the craving for resolution had made him susceptible to any conclusion, however unsatisfactory, that presented itself as one"

Conflict and Opposition

Antagonism"the antagonism between them had reached the stage at which courtesy itself became a form of hostility"
Contention"the contention was not about the matter stated but about a matter neither party was prepared to state"
Dissension"the dissension within the house was conducted at a volume invisible to those outside it"
Enmity"the enmity was old enough to have lost its original cause, which made it, if anything, more intractable"
Acrimony"the acrimony of the exchange was in inverse proportion to the significance of its apparent subject"
Rupture"the rupture, when it came, was the culmination of a thousand small concessions that had never quite been enough"
Irreconcilable"the positions had become irreconcilable not because of their distance but because of the investment made in maintaining it"
Belligerence"the belligerence was performed rather than felt, which made it no less effective as an instrument of intimidation"
Provocation"the provocation was subtle enough to be deniable and precise enough to be unmistakable"
Adversity"the adversity had produced in some a fortitude they had not previously known they possessed"
Subjection"the subjection was voluntary and therefore complete in a way that compulsion could not have achieved"
Tumult"the tumult within him produced, on the surface, a stillness that those who knew him least found reassuring"

Knowledge and Ignorance

Comprehension"the comprehension arrived slowly and then completely, as comprehension does when the mind has been resisting it"
Erudition"the erudition was displayed with the casualness of those who have never needed to consider whether it is welcome"
Oblivion"the oblivion she had sought was not unconsciousness but the suspension of the particular knowledge that troubled her"
Revelation"the revelation was less surprising than the realisation that she had, in some sense, always known it"
Ignorance"the ignorance was not of the innocent variety; it had been cultivated with some effort over a considerable period"
Illumination"the illumination the conversation provided was of the uncomfortable kind that cannot, once received, be returned"
Inscription"the inscription on the stone was worn to illegibility, which seemed appropriate given what it had been placed there to say"
Precognition"the precognition she claimed was, on examination, the ordinary perception of one who pays closer attention than those around her"
Conjecture"the conjecture had hardened, through repetition, into something that resembled certainty in all but origin"
Hypothesis"the hypothesis was presented as a question but carried within it the full weight of a conclusion already reached"
Profundity"the profundity of the silence that followed was the profundity of something that had been waiting a long time to be understood"

Beauty and Ruin

Resplendence"the resplendence of the occasion was in direct proportion to its brevity, which is the common arrangement"
Grandeur"the grandeur of the conception had not survived its execution, which is the common fate of grandeur"
Elegance"the elegance of the solution concealed, as elegance is designed to do, the considerable effort behind it"
Devastation"the devastation was orderly, which made it, in certain respects, more complete than chaos would have been"
Dissolution"the dissolution of what had once seemed permanent produced a revision of everything that had depended upon its permanence"
Desolation"the desolation of the place was of the kind that suggests recent habitation rather than long absence"
Splendour"the splendour of the ruin exceeded, in his estimation, the splendour of whatever it had been before it became one"
Magnificence"the magnificence was real but had been arranged with a precision that made it feel, on reflection, like an argument"
Decrepitude"the decrepitude of the structure was such that it seemed to be held upright by the memory of what it had once been"
Luminosity"the luminosity of her account of the event was inversely proportional to its accuracy"
Radiance"the radiance of the occasion, genuine as it was, did not survive the morning that followed it"
Wreckage"the wreckage of the enterprise was examined by all parties with the thoroughness of those seeking to assign responsibility elsewhere"

Faith and Doubt

Conviction"the conviction with which he held the position was not evidence of its correctness, though it was frequently taken as such"
Scepticism"the scepticism she brought to the account was not hostility but the residue of previous occasions on which she had been too credulous"
Apostasy"the apostasy was gradual and was not recognised as such until it was complete"
Fervour"the fervour of the early years had not diminished so much as redirected itself toward more manageable objects"
Piety"the piety was sincere in its origins and had become, through long practice, indistinguishable from habit"
Heresy"the heresy, uttered once in a moment of honesty, was never repeated but was never forgotten by those who had heard it"
Devotion"the devotion had survived every reasonable test, which suggested it was sustained by something other than reason"
Incredulity"the incredulity with which the news was received was the incredulity of those who had, on some level, expected it"
Veneration"the veneration in which he was held survived the revelation of his flaws, which said something about the depth of the investment"
Perdition"the road to perdition had been paved, in his case, not with good intentions but with careful ones"

Consecration"the consecration of the space was performed without ceremony, which was the most sincere form available"
