States of Mind

1Anguish"the anguish was not loud; it was the kind that occupies a room without announcing itself"
2Consternation"the consternation of the assembled company was expressed, as consternation often is, in complete silence"
3Contemplation"it was in contemplation, not in action, that the weight of the matter made itself fully known"
4Despondency"the despondency was of the quiet variety, the kind that does not announce itself but simply remains"
5Disquiet"a disquiet had taken root in him, small at first, then occupying every quiet moment he permitted himself"
6Elation"the elation was disproportionate to its cause, which told him more about his prior state than about the present one"
7Ennui"the ennui that had settled upon him was not idleness but the exhaustion of one who has ceased to find the world sufficient"
8Equanimity"she received the news with an equanimity that cost her, as equanimity always does, considerably more than it appeared"
9Exhilaration"the exhilaration was brief and therefore precious, as all things are that cannot be prolonged without diminishment"
10Foreboding"a foreboding she could neither name nor dismiss had accompanied her since the letter arrived"
11Indignation"her indignation was righteous but impractical, as indignation tends to be when directed at the irreversible"
12Melancholy"a melancholy, not of circumstance but of constitution, had settled upon him without invitation"
13Perturbation"the perturbation of his composure, so long preserved, was visible only to those who had known him longest"
14Rapture"the rapture lasted only as long as the ignorance that had produced it"
15Repose"the mind, denied its repose, begins to fracture at its quieter edges"
16Resignation"resignation, when it came, arrived not as defeat but as a long-postponed understanding"
17Reverie"she had slipped, without quite intending it, into a reverie from which the afternoon could not recall her"
18Serenity"a serenity had come over her that those who loved her found more troubling than grief"
19Solicitude"her solicitude for his condition exceeded what the occasion seemed, to those outside it, to demand"
20Stupor"a stupor had descended upon the household, thick as the grief that had preceded it"
21Torpor"the torpor of long grief had made even small decisions feel like expeditions into unknown country"
22Trepidation"with a trepidation she had not anticipated, she turned toward what she had long claimed to desire"
23Vexation"his vexation, contained with effort, expressed itself only in the precision of his subsequent silences"
24Weariness"the weariness was not of the body but of the kind that accumulates in those who have hoped too precisely"
25Compunction"compunction, arriving late as it invariably did, found him already committed to the course it counselled against"
26Agitation"the agitation beneath his measured speech was perceptible only in the stillness of his hands"

Human Conduct

1Acquiescence"the acquiescence was swift and therefore alarming to those who had expected resistance"
2Candour"her candour, delivered without cruelty, was the more devastating for its absence of intent"
3Caprice"what appeared to be caprice was, on closer examination, a system too personal to be legible to others"
4Circumspection"circumspection had served him well in every matter except the one in which it was most required"
5Condescension"the condescension was so refined as to be almost indistinguishable from benevolence, which was precisely its intention"
6Contrition"his contrition was visible and, for that reason, slightly suspect to those who knew him best"
7Decorum"decorum was preserved throughout, which is to say that nothing of any consequence was permitted to be said"
8Deference"the deference he showed was genuine, though it was directed at the position rather than the person who occupied it"
9Dissimulation"the dissimulation was accomplished with such ease that she began to doubt whether ease itself was not its own confession"
10Duplicity"the duplicity had been so long sustained that he had ceased to recognise it as such"
11Forbearance"the forbearance she had exercised for so long had been mistaken, by those who benefited from it, for approval"
12Impertinence"the impertinence of the question was exceeded only by the accuracy of its aim"
13Indiscretion"the indiscretion was small but had the particular quality of small indiscretions, which is that they are remembered longest"
14Insolence"the insolence was not in the words but in the composure with which they were delivered"
15Intransigence"her intransigence on this point was the single quality that those who admired her could not admire"
16Magnanimity"the magnanimity of his response cost him nothing, which perhaps explained why it was so readily extended"
17Obstinacy"the obstinacy he displayed was the obstinacy of the man who has been right too often to consider the alternative"
18Perfidy"the perfidy was not of the spectacular kind; it was the slow, domestic variety that leaves no single moment to point at"
19Presumption"the presumption of the request was so remarkable that it silenced, for a moment, even its intended recipient"
20Probity"his probity in financial matters was beyond question, which made the other matters all the more confounding"
21Propriety"propriety demanded one thing; honesty demanded another; she chose, as she always had, the former"
22Rectitude"his rectitude, genuine enough in its origins, had hardened over years into something indistinguishable from severity"
23Reticence"his reticence on the matter was itself a form of testimony, and was understood as such"
24Scruple"the scruple that had prevented him once did not present itself a second time, which is how most things are lost"
25Temerity"the temerity of the proposal was matched only by the sincerity with which it was advanced"
26Vice"the vice was not dramatic but habitual, which made it considerably harder to renounce"
27Virtue"the virtue she had cultivated with such effort had become, in the eyes of others, merely her character"
28Dissipation"the dissipation had been gradual enough that no single evening could be identified as its beginning"
29Cupidity"the cupidity that drove him was dressed, as cupidity invariably is, in the language of necessity"

Fate and Circumstance

1Fate"the fate that had been prepared for him had required no dramatic instrument — only patience and time"
2Fortune"fortune had been inconsistent in its attentions, which he had taken, incorrectly, as a sign of its eventual generosity"
3Misfortune"the misfortune was not in the event itself but in the sequence of smaller misfortunes that had made it inevitable"
4Providence"he attributed to providence what a less partial observer might have attributed to preparation"
5Adversity"the adversity had produced in some a fortitude they had not previously known they possessed"
6Ruin"the ruin had been so long approaching that its arrival was received less with shock than with the relief of conclusion"
7Calamity"the calamity was private, which made it, in the absence of witnesses, no less complete"
8Tribulation"the tribulation had been endured with a steadiness that those who observed it found, privately, excessive"
9Affliction"the affliction was of the spirit, which made those around her uncertain whether sympathy or distance was the more appropriate response"
10Deliverance"the deliverance, when it came, arrived in a form so ordinary that it was almost missed"

Time and Passage

1Ere"ere the morning had fully asserted itself, the decision had already been made and unmade twice"
2Erstwhile"the erstwhile companion, now a stranger in all but name, passed without acknowledgement"
3Evanescent"the evanescent quality of the light at that hour made the garden appear to belong to a different season"
4Henceforth"henceforth, she resolved, the door of that particular consideration would remain closed to her"
5Hitherto"what was hitherto unspoken had accumulated in the room until the air itself felt weighted with it"
6Immemorial"the custom was of immemorial origin, which is to say that no one living could recall either its beginning or its purpose"
7Inexorable"the inexorable passage of the matter toward its conclusion could be observed but not interrupted"
8Interminable"the interminable afternoon had the quality of time that refuses to be consumed by the activities arranged to consume it"
9Lingering"a lingering sense of what had been said accompanied her through the remainder of the evening"
10Perpetual"the perpetual noise of the age had made silence not merely rare but, to certain constitutions, unbearable"
11Posterity"he wrote with an awareness of posterity that those who read him in his lifetime found faintly presumptuous"
12Protracted"the protracted nature of the farewell exceeded the emotional resources of those required to sustain it"
13Remnant"the remnant of his dignity, small but intact, was the only thing he left the room with"
14Transient"the happiness was transient in the manner of all happinesses that are recognised as such while they persist"
15Vestige"the vestige of her former confidence remained in her posture long after it had abandoned her expression"
16Whilst"whilst the others deliberated, she had already arrived at the conclusion they would reach an hour hence"
17Epoch"the epoch demanded a kind of certainty that the most honest among them found themselves unable to supply"
18Imminent"the imminent conclusion of the affair cast a peculiar light over everything that preceded it"

Judgment and Reason

1Abstraction"the abstraction into which he retreated during moments of difficulty had become, over time, his primary residence"
2Acuity"the acuity of his observation was in inverse proportion to his willingness to act upon what it revealed"
3Cogitation"the cogitation lasted through the night and produced, by morning, a conclusion he had held since the previous afternoon"
4Conjecture"the conjecture, offered with the confidence of established fact, was received as such by those predisposed to believe it"
5Deliberation"the deliberation was lengthy, which those who awaited its conclusion took as a sign of care rather than uncertainty"
6Discernment"the discernment she brought to matters of character was not always extended to matters of her own"
7Equivocation"the equivocation was so accomplished that it satisfied, temporarily, both sides of the question it declined to answer"
8Erudition"his erudition was genuine and extensive and served, on certain occasions, as a substitute for understanding"
9Fallacy"the fallacy at the centre of the argument had been visible from the outset to all who had no investment in its conclusion"
10Lucidity"the lucidity of her argument was undeniable and therefore, to those who opposed it, deeply inconvenient"
11Perception"her perception of the difficulty was complete long before she permitted herself to acknowledge it"
12Perspicacity"the perspicacity she brought to others' difficulties abandoned her entirely when applied to her own"
13Precept"the precept had been followed so long it had acquired the authority of instinct rather than instruction"
14Prudence"prudence counselled silence; something older and less advisable counselled otherwise"
15Ratiocination"the ratiocination that had served him in every lesser matter failed him now at the point of greatest consequence"
16Reflection"the reflection came too late to alter the decision and served only to illuminate its cost"
17Sagacity"the sagacity attributed to him was largely the product of a reputation formed before the evidence was available"
18Sophistry"the sophistry was elegant and, to those who found elegant reasoning more persuasive than sound reasoning, entirely convincing"
19Veracity"the veracity of the account was not in question; it was its completeness that gave cause for reservation"
20Profundity"the profundity of the silence that followed was the profundity of something that had been waiting a long time to be understood"

Appearance and Atmosphere

1Air"there was an air about him of someone who had recently made a decision and not yet determined whether to regret it"
2Aspect"the aspect of the house at that hour suggested habitation without warmth, which was accurate"
3Countenance"the countenance he presented to the world was composed with a care that those who loved him found exhausting"
4Gloom"the gloom of the interior was not the gloom of neglect but of deliberate withdrawal from the brightness outside"
5Pallor"the pallor that had settled upon her since the news arrived lent her the appearance of someone already partly absent"
6Prospect"the prospect from the upper window had not changed, which made the change in everything else more visible"
7Radiance"the radiance of the occasion, genuine as it was, did not survive the morning that followed it"
8Shade"she remained in the shade of the conversation, present enough to observe and absent enough to remain unaccountable"
9Penumbra"she stood at the penumbra of the lamplight, neither fully illuminated nor fully absent from the scene"
10Luminescence"the luminescence of the water at that hour suggested an internal source rather than a borrowed one"

Power and Authority

1Allegiance"the allegiance had been transferred so gradually that no moment of betrayal could be identified"
2Ascendancy"the ascendancy of one faction over another had been so gradual that no single moment of defeat could be identified"
3Dominion"the dominion he exercised over the household was absolute and, for that reason, rarely required to be exercised at all"
4Fealty"the fealty he owed was to the principle rather than the person, a distinction that had cost him the person"
5Hegemony"the hegemony had been maintained not by force but by the more durable instrument of assumed consent"
6Imperious"the imperious manner in which the instruction was delivered ensured that its content was almost entirely overlooked"
7Prerogative"she exercised the prerogative of silence with the same authority others brought to speech"
8Sovereignty"the sovereignty of her own judgment was the only sovereignty she had never been persuaded to surrender"
9Subjugation"the subjugation was so long established that the subjugated had ceased to experience it as such"
10Tyranny"the tyranny was of the benevolent variety, which is the most complete because it permits no legitimate grievance"
11Usurpation"the usurpation was accomplished so quietly that those displaced were uncertain, for some time, whether it had occurred"
12Capitulation"the capitulation was dressed as compromise, which satisfied the dignity of all parties without altering the outcome"

Faith and Devotion

1Absolution"the absolution he sought was not available from any of the sources to which he applied for it"
2Apostasy"the apostasy was gradual and was not recognised as such until it was complete"
3Consecration"the consecration of the space was performed without ceremony, which was the most sincere form available"
4Conviction"the conviction with which he held the position was not evidence of its correctness, though it was frequently taken as such"
5Devotion"the devotion had survived every reasonable test, which suggested it was sustained by something other than reason"
6Fervour"the fervour of the early years had not diminished so much as redirected itself toward more manageable objects"
7Heresy"the heresy, uttered once in a moment of honesty, was never repeated but was never forgotten by those who had heard it"
8Incredulity"the incredulity with which the news was received was the incredulity of those who had, on some level, expected it"
9Perdition"the road to perdition had been paved, in his case, not with good intentions but with careful ones"
10Piety"the piety was sincere in its origins and had become, through long practice, indistinguishable from habit"
11Sanctity"the sanctity of the arrangement had been observed for so long that its violation felt less like transgression than like revelation"
12Scepticism"the scepticism she brought to the account was not hostility but the residue of previous occasions on which she had been too credulous"
13Veneration"the veneration in which he was held survived the revelation of his flaws, which said something about the depth of the investment"

Loss and Absence

1Abandonment"the abandonment was not dramatic; it was the slow withdrawal of attention, which is the more thorough kind"
2Bereavement"the bereavement had altered his relationship to time in ways he could not have predicted and could not reverse"
3Bereft"she was bereft not of him but of the version of herself that had existed in relation to him"
4Desolation"the desolation was not in the place but in the certainty that the place would remain when she did not"
5Diminishment"the diminishment of his expectations had been so gradual that he had not noticed it until the expectations were gone entirely"
6Dissolution"the dissolution of what had once seemed permanent produced a revision of everything that had depended upon its permanence"
7Estrangement"the estrangement had occurred without a declared occasion, which made it impossible to appeal"
8Languish"the correspondence had been allowed to languish, and with it the understanding that had depended upon it"
9Privation"the privation was material but its effects were felt most acutely in the curtailment of what had previously been taken for granted"
10Severance"the severance was clean, which is to say it was performed with precision and felt like violence"
11Void"the void left by the departure was disproportionate to what, examined plainly, the presence had actually provided"
12Lament"the lament, private and sustained, had been conducted without witnesses, which made it no less thorough"

Desire and Longing

1Appetite"the appetite for certainty, unmet for so long, had begun to accept probability as a reasonable substitute"
2Ardour"the ardour of his early intention had cooled into something more durable but considerably less useful"
3Aspiration"the aspiration had been noble in its origin and had become, through the passage of time, merely habitual"
4Craving"the craving for resolution had made him susceptible to any conclusion, however unsatisfactory, that presented itself as one"
5Inclination"the inclination toward solitude had been present in him since childhood and had only strengthened with the provision of its alternatives"
6Longing"the longing was retrospective, which made it both more precise and entirely without remedy"
7Pining"the pining was conducted without exhibition, which is the only variety that lasts"
8Predilection"her predilection for the melancholy in music was not a pose; it was a preference formed before she had language for it"
9Yearning"the yearning was not for the thing itself but for the version of herself that had existed when the thing was possible"

Conflict and Opposition

1Acrimony"the acrimony of the exchange was in inverse proportion to the significance of its apparent subject"
2Antagonism"the antagonism between them had reached the stage at which courtesy itself became a form of hostility"
3Belligerence"the belligerence was performed rather than felt, which made it no less effective as an instrument of intimidation"
4Contention"the contention was not about the matter stated but about a matter neither party was prepared to state"
5Dissension"the dissension within the house was conducted at a volume invisible to those outside it"
6Enmity"the enmity was old enough to have lost its original cause, which made it, if anything, more intractable"
7Provocation"the provocation was subtle enough to be deniable and precise enough to be unmistakable"
8Rupture"the rupture, when it came, was the culmination of a thousand small concessions that had never quite been enough"
9Tumult"the tumult within him produced, on the surface, a stillness that those who knew him least found reassuring"

Speech and Expression

1Admonition"the admonition was delivered with sufficient gentleness to be ignored and sufficient precision to be remembered"
2Cadence"the cadence of her speech had the quality of thought arriving in real time rather than being delivered from preparation"
3Discourse"the discourse had been elevated in its subject and disappointing in its conclusions, as elevated discourse often is"
4Effusion"the effusion of feeling, genuine as it was, made those present uncertain how to arrange their own expressions"
5Elegy"what had begun as a letter of explanation had become, by its third page, an elegy"
6Imprecation"the imprecation was whispered rather than spoken, as though volume might make it harder to retract"
7Invective"the invective was delivered calmly, which made it considerably more effective than passion would have allowed"
8Proclamation"the proclamation was made with the conviction of one who had been rehearsing it privately for some considerable time"
9Pronouncement"the pronouncement was final in its form and provisional, as all pronouncements are, in its effect"
10Remonstrance"the remonstrance was heard, acknowledged with apparent attention, and entirely disregarded"
11Soliloquy"the soliloquy, nominally addressed to the company, was in truth addressed to a single pair of ears within it"
12Supplication"the supplication was framed as a request but possessed the gravity of something closer to necessity"
13Utterance"the utterance, once made, could not be recalled; it remained in the room long after the conversation had ended"

1Abyss"the abyss was not beneath him but within him, and far less navigable for that"

4Contrivance"every contrivance of the age conspires against the stillness the mind requires to know itself"
5Edifice"the edifice, already old when his grandfather had known it, seemed to regard him with a settled indifference"
6Enclosure"the enclosure offered safety of the kind that cannot be distinguished, from the inside, from captivity"
7Expanse"the expanse before them offered the particular loneliness of space without destination"
8Firmament"she had looked, on such evenings, to the firmament for the reassurance that the earth withheld"
9Hearth"the hearth had been cold for a season, and the room had taken on the character of what it lacked"
10Hollow"the hollow in the hillside had collected the morning's silence and held it past noon"
11Nave"the nave absorbed their voices and returned them as something closer to supplication than speech"
12Parapet"he leaned upon the parapet and regarded the city below with the detachment of one who had ceased to belong to it"
13Precipice"he stood at a precipice not of stone but of consequence, and found it no less vertiginous for that"
14Sepulchre"the house had become, by degrees and without declaration, a sepulchre for the life once lived within it"
15Threshold"at the threshold, he paused — not from hesitation but from the recognition that entry was irreversible"
16Vale"the vale lay below them, indifferent to the season and to the small figures moving across it"
17Vestibule"the vestibule retained the cold of the morning long after the rest of the house had surrendered it"

1Decrepitude"the decrepitude of the structure was such that it seemed to be held upright by the memory of what it had once been"

4Grandeur"the grandeur of the conception had not survived its execution, which is the common fate of grandeur"
