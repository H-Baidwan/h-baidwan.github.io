Victorian English

Class: Style  ·  VIC

What It Is

Victorian English is the style of abstraction and elegance, to convey meaning in such a tone that the reader begins to perceive the subject as timeless. It often uses elevated vocabulary to add Weight.

The Structure, as defined

1. Nested Clauses

Commas are present in each abstraction, extension, or other approaches used to delay the main meaning of the thought, to have the reader pause at the moment the writer desires.

"The wind, restless as it is, begins to assert itself-"

2. The Delayed Meaning

The Main Subject is introduced early but is starved off(m) the complete reveal towards the end, as it gains Momentum from the Nested Clauses. The delay is introduced before the Main Verb, not after it.

3. Abstraction As The Definer

The Main Subject gets broken into fragments(m) that define the meaning behind it, adding Weight, making the reader visualise it.

4. Final Word

The Final Word of the sentence has to be worthy of all the Momentum brought upon it. The Final Word is the stamp on the paper.(m)

5. Clause Coordination

The Main Subject gets broken into fragments(m), producing a chain(m) of Nested Clauses that describe the Main Subject in different wordings, all expressing it with abstraction, resulting in the reader feeling the Weight behind the thought.

6. The Semicolonial Box

Victorian English uses semicolons in ways standard grammar does not permit, entirely for Pacing.

Use 1 — The Conjunctive Pause

Uses a standard conjunctive adverb inside a Semicolonial Box for Weight.

"; therefore,"
Use 2 — The Transitional Phrase

Crafts a concise phrase for the same purpose as Use 1 inside the Box.

"; and thus,"
Use 3 — The Isolated Phrase

Places a specific phrase that would have been outside the Box in standard grammar inside it, to have the remainder echo louder.(m)

"; yet the wound, remained open"

Illustrative Examples

1. Solitary Observation

The subject stands at a window and observes snowfall.

"There is, in the descent of snow, a quality which the eye receives before the mind has found the language for it; hence, the observer standing at the glass becomes aware not of cold or whiteness but of something altogether quieter, a correction the world makes on such mornings, into a stillness that plain hours are too crowded to permit."

2. Private Grief

The subject writes about losing a sister.

"She did not leave as the dying are sometimes said to leave, by degrees that allow the living to prepare themselves in advance of the loss; and still the absence, arriving without negotiation, without the mercy of gradual withdrawal, became not a wound to be dressed but a silence to be inhabited, the kind that persists in the intervals between thought, where habit continues long after the conditions that produced it have ceased to exist."

3. Attention's Nature

The subject comments on the cost of low value dopamine.

"For it must be observed that the mind, when perpetually summoned from one object to another by the ceaseless provocations of modern contrivance, loses not its capacity for thought, but its rightful opportunity for repose; and thus attention, deprived of the silence in which it gathers strength, becomes scattered, not through moral failing, but through the unrelenting demands placed upon it."

4. Conversation on War

A friend writes: "I cannot make sense of it. Young men sent to fields they have never seen, for causes they were never consulted on. Is there any justification that holds?"

The subject replies:

"I have looked, as you have looked, for the argument that resolves it; wherein the search itself, pursued long enough, becomes the discovery, that we have been looking for justification where there is only explanation, and have mistaken the latter for the former because the grief, were it to go unanswered, would be too heavy to carry without something to set against it."

5. Conversation on Architecture

An architect says: "I worried through the entire construction that the proportions of the eastern facade would overwhelm the entrance. Looking at it now I am not certain my worry was misplaced."

The subject replies:

"Your worry was not misplaced so much as premature; yet the facade, which you feared would overwhelm, does instead what only the most considered structures permit themselves to do, it announces rather than dominates, it justifies its own scale in the relationship between the stone and the light it receives at this hour, which I do not believe you entirely planned, and which is, for that reason, the most honest thing about it."

Definitions

1. "Final Word" — "Heavy"

A Final Word is a word that is lexical, one that carries standalone meaning without relying on function words such as intensifiers, comparatives, or connectives to complete it.

A lexical word can also be an ordinary word in everyday life but has the potential of sounding Heavy if given the right Momentum as described in the Structure. An example for a word like "Jurisdiction":

"The courtroom, filled of paperwork from centuries ago, a verdict on its run, delivered by none other than the Jurisdiction."

2. "Elevated Vocabulary"

Elevated vocabulary refers to words consistent with the literary tradition of Victorian prose, words that carry formal Weight without belonging to a specialist domain such as law, medicine, or science.

A non-exhaustive reference list is provided in Appendix VIC. Words outside the Appendix are not automatically disqualified. The Assessor evaluates whether the word serves the sentence's Weight in a manner consistent with the tradition demonstrated in the Illustrative Examples and Definition 1.

3. "Qualifier"

A Qualifier is one that is present in one of the six subsections in the Structure. A Qualifier is what you look for when evaluating the Victorian English.

4. Referenced Terms

"Main Subject"

The topic the sentence is built around: The entity, concept, or abstraction that the Nested Clauses serve to describe.

"Main Verb"

The action or state attributed to the Main Subject. Its arrival completes the sentence's primary meaning.

"Nest"

The singular form of Nested Clauses. Refers to a single clause within the sequence defined in Structure 1.

"Momentum"

The cumulative effect produced when one or more of the six points from the Structure are present and building toward the Final Word. Not all six are required. A single well-executed Qualifier, consistent with the VIC Doctrine, creates Momentum. The more points present and correctly applied, the greater the Momentum the Final Word receives.

"Weight"

The result of Momentum meeting a lexical Final Word. Momentum without a lexical Final Word dissipates. A lexical Final Word without Momentum is only a word. Weight is what occurs when both conditions are met simultaneously.

"Pacing"

The regulation of the reader's cognitive tempo through structural and punctuation choices. Defined in full under the Pacing Doctrine.
